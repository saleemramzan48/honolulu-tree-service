What to Do When a Tree Falls on Your House in Honolulu

A tree falling on a home is among the most jarring and disorienting property emergencies a Honolulu homeowner can experience. Whether it happened during a nighttime storm while you were asleep or in the middle of the day with no warning, the immediate hours after impact are critical — for safety, for insurance documentation, and for limiting secondary damage.

This guide walks through every step, in order, so you know exactly what to do even in the adrenaline-charged moments after impact.

The First 60 Minutes — Your Priorities in Order

1

Get everyone out and away from the impact zone

If the tree has struck the building while people are inside, evacuate immediately. A partially penetrated structure is not stable — what hasn't collapsed can still shift. Move all occupants (including pets) to the street or a neighboring property at safe distance.

2

Call 911 if anyone is injured or trapped

Honolulu Fire Department has tree removal and structural rescue capability. If anyone is injured or trapped, call 911 immediately. Property concerns come second to personal safety.

3

Check for power line contact — call HECO if present

Look at the tree from a safe distance. Is it in contact with overhead power lines? If yes, call HECO at (808) 548-7311 immediately. Keep everyone away from both the tree and any utility lines near the impact. Downed or contacted lines may be energized.

4

Photograph everything before anything is touched

Before the tree service crew, before the roofer, before anyone touches anything — photograph the scene comprehensively. The failure point where the tree broke or uprooted. Every point of structural contact. The interior damage visible from doorways or windows. The full extent of the tree from the base to the impact. Multiple angles.

5

Call your homeowner's insurance company

Open a claim immediately. You do not need to know all the details or have the tree removed before calling. Early notification establishes the timeline and gets the claim process moving. Ask your insurer whether they have preferred tree service or roofing contractors they want you to use — some policies have requirements that affect reimbursement.

6

Call for emergency tree removal

Once the safety situation is assessed and documentation is in hand, call a licensed emergency tree service. The tree on your home is a continuing hazard — the sooner a professional crew can stabilize the situation and remove the tree, the sooner you can assess and begin repairing the structural damage it caused.

7

Protect exposed interior areas

If the tree has breached your roof or walls, apply tarps or plastic sheeting over the exposed interior while you wait for the tree crew. Honolulu can receive rainfall at any time, and water intrusion into a compromised structure causes rapidly escalating damage. Even a light tarp applied quickly can prevent significant additional loss.

Immediate Safety Priorities

The instinct when a tree falls on your home is to go examine the damage immediately. Fight that instinct. Here's why the hazard extends further than the visible impact:

  • The tree may not be done moving. A tree resting against a structure is under tension — as wood dries, as the structure shifts, as the wind affects the canopy, the load distribution changes. A tree that appears stable can shift suddenly.
  • Secondary branches can release. Large trees under stress can drop secondary branches without warning — the same mechanical forces that caused the main failure are still present in the rest of the tree.
  • Root balls are unpredictable. When a tree uproots, the root ball creates a void in the soil. As the tree settles, the root ball can drop back, shift, or cause further ground movement. Stay away from the base of uprooted trees.
  • Structural integrity of the building is unknown. A tree impact can compromise roof trusses, wall studs, or foundation connections in ways that aren't visible from the outside. Don't assume a room that "looks fine" is safe to enter without structural assessment.

Electrical and Utility Hazards

Electrical hazards are the most serious life-safety consideration after a tree falls on a Honolulu home. The specific scenarios to understand:

Tree contacting overhead power lines: Call HECO at (808) 548-7311. Do not approach the tree or the lines. Current can travel through wet wood, through the ground around the tree's root zone, and through standing water. The "safe" zone around a downed or contacted line is much larger than most people assume.

Tree impact on electrical service entrance: If the tree struck the area where your home's electrical service comes in (typically at the meter and main panel), treat the entire building's electrical system as compromised. Do not use any electrical outlets or switches until an electrician has assessed and cleared the panel and service entrance.

Gas lines: If you smell gas or suspect a gas line has been damaged by root movement or ground displacement, evacuate and call 911 and your gas provider. Hawaiian Electric and Hawaiʻi Gas have emergency lines for exactly this situation.

Document Before Anything Is Moved

This step cannot be overstated. Insurance adjusters and contractors who arrive after the fact are working from your documentation. The more complete it is, the better your claim outcome.

What to capture with photos and video:

  • The failure point at the base or main trunk of the tree — where it broke, uprooted, or split
  • Every contact point between the tree and the structure
  • Structural damage to roof, walls, windows from multiple angles
  • Interior damage visible without entering compromised areas (photograph from doorways or unaffected rooms)
  • The full tree from base to tip to establish size
  • The surrounding property context — adjacent structures, driveways, neighboring trees
  • Any damage to vehicles, fences, or outbuildings
  • Time stamps on all images (your phone does this automatically)

Also save: weather service reports for the day of the event, any local news coverage, and any photos you may have taken of your property in normal conditions (establishing what it looked like before the event).

Call Your Insurance Company

Most homeowner's insurance policies cover emergency tree removal when the fallen tree has damaged a covered structure. The key word is "damaged" — insurers generally do not cover the cost to remove a tree that fell into your yard without hitting anything. Understanding your policy's specific language before an event occurs is valuable.

When you call to open a claim:

  • Provide the date and time of the event
  • Describe what was damaged (structure, vehicle, fence)
  • Confirm you have photo documentation
  • Ask specifically whether there are preferred contractors you should use for tree removal and structural repair
  • Ask about your deductible and coverage limits for this type of claim
  • Get a claim number and the adjuster's direct contact information

For detailed coverage guidance specific to Hawaii, see Is Emergency Tree Removal Covered by Homeowners Insurance in Hawaiʻi?

Call for Emergency Tree Removal

When you call a tree service, have the following information ready:

  • Your address and neighborhood in Honolulu
  • Whether the tree is on the structure (yes/no)
  • Whether there is power line contact
  • Approximate tree size
  • Whether access to the property is available (driveway clear, gate access, etc.)

A licensed emergency crew will assess the situation on arrival, develop a removal sequence, and remove the tree from your structure in sections — using rigging and controlled cuts to prevent additional structural damage during the removal process. This is specialized work. The goal is not just to remove the tree, but to remove it without turning a damaged roof into a destroyed roof.

Temporary Protection While You Wait

Honolulu's climate means that any exposed interior is vulnerable to rapid water damage. Even a brief rain shower can deposit significant water into a compromised roof section. While you wait for the tree removal crew and subsequent structural repair:

  • Place tarps or plastic sheeting over any interior areas below the breach point
  • Use buckets or containers to catch any active water intrusion
  • Remove electronics, important documents, and valuables from the impacted area if it's safe to do so
  • Do not enter rooms directly beneath the impact point or where ceiling integrity is uncertain

Who Pays? When the Tree Came from Your Neighbor's Yard

This is one of the most common questions after a tree damage event in Honolulu — and the answer is not what most people expect.

In Hawaii, as in most states, each property owner is generally responsible for damage caused by trees originating on their property — but the question of liability is more nuanced than that in practice. Key factors:

  • If the neighbor's tree was known to be dead, diseased, or dangerous — and you notified them in writing before the failure — the neighbor may bear greater liability for the resulting damage.
  • If the tree appeared healthy and fell due to storm conditions, your own homeowner's insurance will typically cover the structural damage to your home, regardless of where the tree originated.
  • If the neighbor's tree fell on your car, your auto insurance (comprehensive coverage) generally covers that — not the neighbor's liability policy.

The practical advice: file a claim with your own insurance first. Your insurer's subrogation process will pursue recovery from the neighbor's insurer if there is a valid claim against the neighbor. You don't need to manage that dispute directly. Document the communication: if you had previously notified your neighbor about a tree that you believed was hazardous, locate any written record of that communication and provide it to your insurer.
