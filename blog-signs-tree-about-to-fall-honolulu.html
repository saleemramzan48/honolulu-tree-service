Signs a Tree Is About to Fall in Honolulu — What to Watch For

By the Oahu Tree Rescue Team  ·  March 2025  ·  8 min read

⚠️

In This Article

  1. The 7 Warning Signs
  2. Honolulu-Specific Risk Factors
  3. What to Do When You See Warning Signs
  4. When to Call a Professional

Most tree failures in Honolulu don't happen without warning. The problem is knowing what to look for — and understanding which signs mean "schedule an assessment" versus which ones mean "keep everyone away from that tree today."

You don't need to be an arborist to recognize the most significant warning signs. You do need to know what you're looking at. This guide covers the seven most important visual indicators of tree failure risk that Honolulu homeowners can identify themselves — with clear guidance on which require urgent action.

The 7 Warning Signs

🔴 1. Visible Root Damage or Soil Uplift Call Today

If you can see exposed roots that have been damaged by recent construction, trenching, or paving, or if you notice the soil rising or cracking on one side of the tree base, the tree's anchoring system may be compromised. This is the highest-urgency warning sign available to the naked eye. A tree with compromised roots can fail at any time, with no additional warning — even in calm weather. Do not wait for the next windstorm to confirm this diagnosis.

🔴 2. Visible Lean That Has Changed Call Today

All trees lean to some degree — often in response to light availability or prevailing wind. What matters is change. If a tree that has stood straight for years is now noticeably leaning, or if a tree that always had a slight lean has become dramatically more tilted, the root system is failing. This sign combined with soil uplift on the opposite side is an imminent failure situation. Take it seriously immediately.

🔴 3. Fungal Growth at the Base or Major Roots Urgent Assessment

Mushrooms or shelf fungi (bracket fungi) growing at the base of a tree or on exposed roots are almost always a sign of internal decay. The fungi you see on the outside are the fruiting bodies of a wood-rot organism that has likely been working through the tree's root system and lower trunk for months or years. The visible fungi represent the tip of a much larger problem. How serious it is depends on which decay fungus is present and how far it has progressed — only a professional with a sounding mallet or resistograph can determine that. But surface fungi at the base always warrant an assessment, not monitoring.

🟡 4. Large Dead Branches in the Canopy Schedule Soon

Dead branches — identifiable by their lack of leaves during the growing season, brittle texture, and grayish-brown bark — can fall at any time without wind. They don't have the elasticity of living wood to flex and hold on. A single dead branch the size of your forearm can break a windshield or injure a person below. Large dead branches (anything over 2 inches in diameter) over occupied areas, driveways, or neighboring property should be addressed promptly. In Honolulu, arborists call this "widow maker" material — and it's a common cause of injury between storms.

🟡 5. Cracks in the Trunk or Major Branch Unions Schedule Soon

Deep cracks (not surface checks in the bark) in the main trunk or at branch unions are structural warning signs. Look for cracks that run vertically along the trunk, or opening cracks at the "V" where two main branches meet — especially if you can see exposed wood rather than covered bark at the crack edge. These are often accompanied by "included bark" — where bark has been trapped inside the union rather than forming a solid wood-to-wood connection. Included bark unions are significantly weaker than open-bark unions and are a common failure point in Honolulu wind events.

🟡 6. Dieback in the Upper Canopy Schedule Assessment

When a tree begins to die, it often starts at the top — a pattern called "top-down dieback" or "crown dieback." If you notice the upper portion of a large tree losing leaves, developing dead branch sections, or looking sparse and unhealthy while the lower canopy appears fine, the tree may be experiencing significant root stress, vascular disease, or systemic decline. Top-down dieback in Honolulu's climate is frequently associated with root-zone issues, including root rot, soil compaction from nearby construction, or soil flooding from altered drainage patterns. Schedule a professional assessment.

🟡 7. History of Storm Damage or Previous Heavy Pruning Monitor Closely

Trees that have survived previous storms don't necessarily emerge from them undamaged. Internal cracks, compression wood, and partial root failure can occur in a storm without causing the tree to fall — but leave it more vulnerable to the next event. Similarly, trees that have been improperly topped or heavily cut in the past may have developed internal decay in the wounds, and the fast-growing regrowth is structurally weak. If a tree on your property has been through documented storm damage or was aggressively pruned in the past, have it assessed before each storm season.

Honolulu-Specific Risk Factors

Beyond the standard warning signs above, several factors specific to Honolulu properties increase tree risk in ways that a mainland-focused inspection might miss:

  • Hillside root systems (Diamond Head, Mānoa, Nuuanu). Trees on slopes develop asymmetric root systems that anchor differently than flat-ground trees. Uphill roots carry more load, and erosion on the uphill side can compromise that anchoring without any visible surface change.
  • Soil saturation from Kona lows. Winter Kona low events can saturate Honolulu soils to a degree not seen in normal trade wind weather. Saturated soil reduces root anchoring capacity dramatically — trees that stood fine in normal conditions can uproot when soil turns to mud. If your property received prolonged heavy rain, have higher-risk trees checked after the soil dries.
  • Construction root damage. Honolulu's active development scene — new buildings in Kakaʻako, infrastructure work in downtown, road widening — frequently encroaches on the root zones of established trees on adjacent private property. Root damage from trenching or excavation may not manifest as visible failure symptoms for 1–3 years, and by then the tree is a hidden liability.
  • Coconut and Norfolk Island pine crown stability. Both species can fail catastrophically with little visible warning — coconuts can drop full crowns (not just fronds), and Norfolk Island pines have failed completely at the base with no prior visible lean. Both deserve annual inspection if they're positioned over occupied areas.

What to Do When You See Warning Signs

The key question when you spot a warning sign is: what's below this tree if it fails? A tree showing multiple warning signs over an empty lawn is a different risk priority than the same tree positioned over your master bedroom or your neighbor's driveway.

Immediately after spotting warning signs:

  1. Stop using the area below the tree. Don't park vehicles there, don't let children play beneath it, don't allow regular foot traffic.
  2. Photograph what you're seeing. Date-stamped photos of the warning signs are useful documentation for both the arborist's assessment and any subsequent insurance or liability conversation.
  3. Call a tree professional. Not a general handyman — a licensed tree service or certified arborist. Describe what you're seeing specifically: "I see mushrooms growing at the base of a large monkeypod tree about 40 feet tall, positioned about 15 feet from my house" gives a professional enough information to triage the urgency of the call.

When to Call a Professional — and When to Call Right Now

Call right now (emergency): Any tree that is actively leaning — especially with soil uplift on the opposite side. Any tree that shows active splitting sounds or visible movement. Any tree that has lost major branches in the last 24 hours and still has hanging material overhead. Call (808) 376-2857.

Schedule urgent assessment (within a week): Fungal growth at the base. Visible trunk cracks or included bark splits. Crown dieback in a large tree over or near a structure.

Schedule seasonal assessment: Dead branches in the canopy. History of storm damage or previous heavy pruning. Annual pre-storm season review for any high-value or high-risk tree on the property.
